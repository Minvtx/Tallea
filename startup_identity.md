# Startup Identity

## Core Identity
- **Name:** Tallea
- **Tagline:** Know your fit before you buy.
- **Category:** Fit intelligence for online apparel commerce
- **One-sentence company description:** Tallea is a Buenos Aires-born startup that helps shoppers build a reusable body-based fit profile and uses it to improve size recommendations in online apparel stores.
- **Public promise:** Make online clothing purchases feel less like guessing and more like choosing with evidence.

## Product Framing
- **What the product appears to do:** Tallea guides a shopper through a phone-camera fit profile, adds basic fit preferences, then shows likely size and fit confidence inside participating online stores.
- **What the product actually helps solve:** It reduces the gap between messy size labels, real bodies, garment-specific cuts, and low-confidence purchase decisions.
- **Who it serves first:** Argentine and regional direct-to-consumer apparel brands selling fitted categories through their own storefronts, Instagram-led funnels, and lightweight ecommerce stacks.
- **Initial commercial wedge:** Mid-market womenswear and denim-adjacent brands with high fit anxiety, manual customer-service sizing questions, and painful exchange workflows.
- **Why users might care:** Users want a practical answer before checkout: not a perfect body scan, but a more credible sense of whether this garment will fit them in this brand.
- **Why merchants/platforms might care:** Merchants want fewer abandoned carts, fewer avoidable exchanges, better sizing support, and a way to learn which products create fit confusion.

## Product Boundary at Day 0
- **Works reliably now:** Guided profile creation under decent lighting, basic body-measurement estimation, preference capture, and confidence guidance for a narrow set of fitted garments when merchant size data is clean.
- **Works inconsistently now:** Denim, trousers, stretch fabrics, garments with loose or oversized cuts, brands with vanity sizing, and catalogs where measurements live in images, PDFs, or customer-service memory.
- **Does not work yet:** Universal size prediction, true virtual try-on, reliable cross-brand garment comparison, automated catalog cleanup at scale, or fit confidence for every body type and clothing category.
- **Trust boundary:** Tallea can recommend with confidence ranges; it cannot honestly promise certainty.

## Market Position
- **Current stage:** Day 0 public launch identity with a working fit-profile flow, a small controlled user beta, and a few merchant conversations that are more advanced than the public product suggests.
- **Why now:** Apparel ecommerce is mature enough that merchants feel the cost of returns and exchange friction, but sizing infrastructure remains primitive, especially across Latin American brands with inconsistent local size conventions.
- **What market gap it is entering:** Tallea sits between static size charts, customer-service fit advice, and heavy enterprise personalization tools that are too expensive or too complex for most regional apparel brands.
- **Why this is hard:** The product depends on body input, garment data, user trust, merchant cooperation, and subjective fit preference all behaving well enough at the same time.
- **Why it may matter more than it first appears:** A fit assistant can become a structured fit-data layer for merchants, marketplaces, returns analysis, catalog normalization, and platform-level commerce intelligence.

## Brand and Public Voice
- **Brand tone:** Precise, calm, practical, and quietly ambitious.
- **Public personality:** Useful before visionary; technical without becoming cold; honest about confidence without sounding uncertain.
- **Style of communication:** Short claims, visible limits, concrete examples, and preference for "likely fit" language over absolute promises.
- **What kind of confidence the brand projects:** Tallea sounds like a serious product team solving a daily commerce problem with measurable methods.
- **What kind of uncertainty it hides:** Whether enough users will trust a body-based profile, whether merchants will provide usable catalog data, and whether the business is a shopper tool, merchant product, or infrastructure layer.

## Strategic Elasticity
- **Adjacent opportunities already visible:** Brand-specific fit guidance, customer-service automation, return reason analysis, catalog measurement cleanup, checkout conversion tools, cross-border size translation, and merchant fit dashboards.
- **What the team says they are building:** A reusable fit profile that helps people buy clothes online with more confidence.
- **What they may actually be building without fully realizing it:** A shared fit intelligence layer that maps bodies, preferences, garment data, and purchase outcomes across commerce systems.
- **What kind of external interest could distort the company’s direction:** Retailers may want a white-label conversion widget, marketplaces may want standardized fit data, and investors may push the cleaner B2B infrastructure story before user trust is earned.

## Constraints
- **What this startup is not:** It is not a fashion social network, a generic AI shopping assistant, a hardware scanner, or a magical virtual try-on company.
- **What would make it feel too generic:** Framing it as "AI for ecommerce," ignoring garment-category limits, or treating fit as only a checkout optimization problem.
- **What would make it feel too magical:** Claiming perfect sizing, body certainty from one photo, instant interoperability across all stores, or reliable prediction without merchant data.
- **What should remain unresolved at Day 0:** Whether the user profile or merchant-side signal becomes the core asset, how much privacy friction users will tolerate, whether data defensibility is real, and how far the company can drift toward infrastructure before betraying its public promise.

## Summary for System Use
Tallea is a Buenos Aires-origin fit-intelligence startup focused first on Argentine and regional apparel brands where sizing anxiety is concrete, especially fitted womenswear and denim-adjacent categories. Its public promise is shopper confidence, but its early commercial pull comes from merchants who want fewer abandoned carts, fewer exchanges, and better fit data. At Day 0 the product can produce useful confidence signals in narrow conditions, but it cannot support universal fit claims. The core instability is strategic: Tallea may become a trusted consumer fit profile, a merchant conversion tool, or a broader fit-data infrastructure layer.
