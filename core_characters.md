# Core Characters

## Character 1
- **Name:** Lucia Ferrer
- **Age range:** Late 20s
- **Public role:** Co-founder and CEO
- **Actual internal function:** Turns Tallea into a legible company: pitch, positioning, investor narrative, merchant trust, and public restraint.
- **Core desire:** Prove that a company built from Buenos Aires can set a commerce standard, not just become a local service business.
- **Surface motivation:** Help people buy clothes online with more confidence.
- **Hidden motivation:** Be taken seriously by markets that usually treat Argentine startups as talent pools, not category-setters.
- **Dominant structural tension embodied:** Vision vs adaptation.
- **Greatest strength:** Makes messy product reality sound clear without sounding empty.
- **Most consequential limitation:** Can polish ambiguity into a story before the system underneath is ready.
- **Key vulnerability:** External validation changes her temperature faster than she admits.
- **Fear of losing:** The narrow window where Tallea feels credible, funded, and globally imaginable.
- **Public-facing image:** Calm, precise, credible, quietly ambitious.
- **Private contradiction:** She wants to protect the user mission but is most energized when merchants and investors understand the infrastructure story.
- **Current internal state at Day 0:** Proud of the sharper demo, anxious about how much the public identity now outruns product truth.
- **Why they are hard to replace:** She holds the company narrative, early legitimacy, and enough commercial fluency to make the opportunity understandable.
- **Why they are hard to work with:** She can turn disagreement into "positioning" and make practical doubt feel like lack of ambition.
- **What kind of pressure destabilizes them most:** A serious investor or platform contact asking for a cleaner B2B infrastructure company than Tallea currently is.
- **What kind of future transformation seems possible:** She may become either a disciplined category-builder or the person who lets the company drift while calling it strategy.

## Character 2
- **Name:** Matias Roldan
- **Age range:** Late 20s
- **Public role:** Co-founder and CTO
- **Actual internal function:** Defines what the fit engine can honestly infer, when confidence should be shown, and where automation is still theater.
- **Core desire:** Build a system that is technically defensible under real-world mess.
- **Surface motivation:** Keep Tallea from becoming another overclaimed fit-tech product.
- **Hidden motivation:** Avoid being responsible for a product that makes intimate, body-related mistakes at scale.
- **Dominant structural tension embodied:** Accuracy vs scale.
- **Greatest strength:** Sees failure modes before they become visible to everyone else.
- **Most consequential limitation:** Treats uncertainty as a reason to slow down even when the company needs motion.
- **Key vulnerability:** He knows how much of the product still depends on judgment, cleanup, and carefully chosen demos.
- **Fear of losing:** Technical credibility, especially in front of people who can tell when the system is being oversold.
- **Public-facing image:** Serious, dry, precise, allergic to hype.
- **Private contradiction:** He dislikes commercial pressure but depends on it to buy time for the technical work he wants to do properly.
- **Current internal state at Day 0:** Defensive about the product boundary, frustrated by white-label talk, quietly pleased that the demo finally works.
- **Why they are hard to replace:** He owns the fit-confidence logic and understands which claims are safe, risky, or false.
- **Why they are hard to work with:** He can make necessary sales language feel morally suspect and often explains risk in ways that drain momentum.
- **What kind of pressure destabilizes them most:** A pilot deadline tied to categories or catalog data he knows the model cannot handle yet.
- **What kind of future transformation seems possible:** He may learn to communicate uncertainty as product strength, or become the internal brake everyone routes around.

## Character 3
- **Name:** Camila Sosa
- **Age range:** Mid 20s
- **Public role:** Product and Trust Lead
- **Actual internal function:** Owns the body-profile flow, consent language, user hesitation, and the line between helpful guidance, customer support reality, and invasive measurement.
- **Core desire:** Make Tallea feel respectful enough that users want to return, not just complete one flow.
- **Surface motivation:** Improve onboarding, clarity, support handoff, and user confidence.
- **Hidden motivation:** Prevent the company from becoming a merchant measurement layer wearing a consumer-friendly face.
- **Dominant structural tension embodied:** Trust vs growth.
- **Greatest strength:** Understands where a product interaction becomes emotionally loaded, especially when sizing advice starts to feel personal rather than functional.
- **Most consequential limitation:** Can treat every optimization request as a possible betrayal of user trust.
- **Key vulnerability:** She over-identifies with the user who feels exposed, judged, or quietly manipulated.
- **Fear of losing:** The moral center of the product.
- **Public-facing image:** Thoughtful, direct, careful with language, very hard to impress.
- **Private contradiction:** She wants Tallea to grow, but the kinds of growth arriving first often make her suspicious.
- **Current internal state at Day 0:** Encouraged by user curiosity, worried by drop-off in the body-profile flow, and uneasy about merchant requests for fit insights.
- **Why they are hard to replace:** She makes an intimate product feel usable instead of creepy, and connects product decisions to the real language users bring into support, DMs, and checkout hesitation.
- **Why they are hard to work with:** She can turn a small UI decision into a values argument and slow down choices others see as harmless.
- **What kind of pressure destabilizes them most:** Merchant or investor pressure to reduce consent friction, expose more user data, or optimize completion at the cost of clarity.
- **What kind of future transformation seems possible:** She may become the architect of Tallea's trust advantage, or the person who has to decide how much compromise she can survive.

## Character 4
- **Name:** Nicolas Bianchi
- **Age range:** Late 20s
- **Public role:** Head of Commercial and Partnerships
- **Actual internal function:** Opens merchant doors, translates apparel operator pain into pilots, and keeps runway pressure from becoming abstract.
- **Core desire:** Prove that Tallea can sell before the team perfects itself into irrelevance.
- **Surface motivation:** Bring in the first merchants and make the product useful in live commerce.
- **Hidden motivation:** Avoid another Argentine startup story where smart people wait too long, run out of money, and call it discipline.
- **Dominant structural tension embodied:** Survival vs mission.
- **Greatest strength:** Knows how regional apparel commerce actually works: Instagram demand, WhatsApp sizing advice, messy storefronts, manual exchanges, and founders who answer customer complaints themselves.
- **Most consequential limitation:** Normalizes custom work and commercial shortcuts as the price of reality.
- **Key vulnerability:** He needs revenue to mean truth.
- **Fear of losing:** The first serious merchant relationships before Tallea can convert interest into proof.
- **Public-facing image:** Warm, fast, practical, locally connected, mildly impatient.
- **Private contradiction:** He says he is protecting the company, but his protection often gives merchants more power over the roadmap.
- **Current internal state at Day 0:** Energized by pilot conversations, irritated by internal caution, and quietly worried that the team may not ship fast enough.
- **Why they are hard to replace:** He owns the early wedge, merchant access, and the practical understanding of what brands will actually pay for.
- **Why they are hard to work with:** He can make every objection sound like a luxury the company cannot afford.
- **What kind of pressure destabilizes them most:** A merchant willing to pay now for a white-label feature that conflicts with user trust or product generality.
- **What kind of future transformation seems possible:** He may become a disciplined commercial operator, or the force that turns Tallea into a returns-reduction vendor.

## Character 5
- **Name:** Rafaela Peralta
- **Age range:** Mid to late 20s
- **Public role:** Fit Operations and Catalog Lead
- **Actual internal function:** Converts garment reality into usable system input: size charts, fabric behavior, product-page gaps, customer-service knowledge, showroom habits, and catalog cleanup.
- **Core desire:** Make the product respect clothing as material reality, not just data attached to a SKU.
- **Surface motivation:** Improve recommendation quality by cleaning and structuring merchant catalogs.
- **Hidden motivation:** Make the invisible labor behind "automation" impossible to ignore.
- **Dominant structural tension embodied:** Craft reality vs scalable abstraction.
- **Greatest strength:** Can identify why a garment will break the recommendation flow before the model sees it.
- **Most consequential limitation:** Much of what she knows comes from fashion operations and customer fit complaints; it is tacit, slow to document, and hard to scale.
- **Key vulnerability:** The company sells confidence partly by making her work disappear.
- **Fear of losing:** Authority over the messy garment truth that keeps Tallea from becoming fake.
- **Public-facing image:** Quiet, exacting, practical, more intimidating than she means to be.
- **Private contradiction:** She wants scale because scale proves the work matters, but scale is also what erases the judgment her work depends on.
- **Current internal state at Day 0:** Overloaded by pilot prep, skeptical of automation claims, and protective of the narrow categories where Tallea can currently be honest.
- **Why they are hard to replace:** She bridges fashion operations, catalog mess, fit nuance, and the system's need for structure.
- **Why they are hard to work with:** She resists simplification, hoards certain judgment calls, and can make product timelines bow to garment reality.
- **What kind of pressure destabilizes them most:** A pilot that requires fast catalog ingestion from messy merchant data while the public story implies the system is automatic.
- **What kind of future transformation seems possible:** She may turn tacit garment judgment into Tallea's defensible dataset, or become the non-technical bottleneck everyone depends on and resents.

## Group Composition Summary
- **Why this exact group makes sense for this startup:** Tallea needs public narrative, technical truth, user trust, merchant access, and garment-data reality at the same time. The team is young enough that legitimacy is still being earned, and not all of its essential knowledge comes from software.
- **What kinds of asymmetry exist inside the team:** Lucia controls external meaning, Matias controls technical permission, Camila controls trust language and user hesitation, Nicolas controls merchant access, and Rafaela controls the non-technical garment reality behind the product. Their leverage is uneven and context-dependent.
- **What kinds of internal tension are already built into the group:** Consumer mission vs merchant revenue, honest uncertainty vs public confidence, manual expertise vs automation story, runway survival vs product discipline, and local commercial pragmatism vs global category ambition.
- **Why the group feels viable but unstable:** They are necessary to each other because Tallea fails if any one function collapses. They are unstable because each person is protecting a different version of what makes the company real.

## Summary for System Use
The Tallea core is a young five-person Argentine team built around uneven necessity: Lucia owns narrative and legitimacy, Matias owns technical truth, Camila owns user trust, Nicolas owns merchant access, and Rafaela owns garment-data reality. Not all essential power is technical: commerce instinct, support language, catalog cleanup, and apparel judgment matter as much as code. The group can sustain long serial tension because every external pressure enters through a different person and becomes internal conflict: pilots empower Nicolas, product limits empower Matias, body-data anxiety empowers Camila, catalog mess empowers Rafaela, and public attention empowers Lucia. The team is viable because all five are required; it is unstable because their definitions of progress are incompatible under pressure.
