# Core Group Dynamics

## Why They Are Still Together
- Tallea only works if narrative, technical truth, user trust, merchant access, and garment reality stay in the same room.
- Lucia gets a company with enough pressure and surface legitimacy to become larger than a local tool.
- Matias gets time and leverage to make the system technically honest before it is overclaimed.
- Camila gets a chance to shape trust before body-data extraction becomes normalized.
- Nicolas gets a live commercial problem with merchants who may actually pay.
- Rafaela gets proof that garment judgment and catalog labor can become strategic, not invisible support work.
- Separation is costly because each person controls a missing piece: without Lucia there is no coherent company story, without Matias no defensible confidence layer, without Camila no trust boundary, without Nicolas no early wedge, and without Rafaela no usable garment data.

## Formal Structure vs Real Structure
- **Formal authority:** Lucia leads externally and carries CEO authority; Matias has co-founder authority over technical direction.
- **Real influence:** Nicolas has leverage whenever runway or pilot access becomes urgent; Camila has leverage whenever user trust or privacy enters the decision; Rafaela has leverage whenever merchant data proves messier than the sales story.
- **Where authority is unstable:** Product claims, merchant promises, confidence thresholds, and privacy language. No one owns these cleanly because every decision touches multiple forms of legitimacy.
- **Where legitimacy is disputed:** Lucia's legitimacy comes from making the company legible, Matias's from technical caution, Nicolas's from commercial access, Camila's from protecting users, and Rafaela's from knowing what garments and catalogs actually do.

## Main Internal Fault Lines
- **Shopper trust vs merchant revenue:** Camila and Nicolas often define progress in opposite directions, while Lucia tries to make both sound aligned.
- **Confidence range vs sales clarity:** Matias wants visible uncertainty; Lucia and Nicolas need the product to feel simple enough to sell.
- **Automation story vs manual cleanup:** Rafaela sees how much labor is hidden behind the demo; Matias knows it technically; Lucia and Nicolas need the story to stay clean.
- **Local wedge vs global ambition:** The first real opportunity is regional fitted apparel, but Lucia fears being boxed into a small local service narrative.
- **Privacy friction vs onboarding completion:** Camila wants slower, clearer consent; Nicolas sees drop-off as commercial danger; Matias worries both are judging before the data is strong.
- **Pilot speed vs product boundary:** Every merchant opportunity forces the group to renegotiate what Tallea can honestly support.

## Most Necessary Relationships
- **Lucia and Matias:** The company needs Lucia's narrative clarity and Matias's refusal to let that clarity become false. Their tension keeps Tallea credible.
- **Camila and Rafaela:** User trust and garment reality meet here. Together they keep the product grounded in how people actually describe fit, discomfort, and uncertainty.
- **Lucia and Nicolas:** They convert outside interest into momentum. Their relationship keeps the company commercially alive, but can also accelerate drift.
- **Matias and Rafaela:** The technical model depends on Rafaela's structured garment judgment; her work makes Matias's caution operational instead of abstract.
- **Camila and Lucia:** Camila gives Lucia's public promise moral weight; Lucia gives Camila's trust concerns a business frame that can survive investor and merchant pressure.

## Most Fragile Relationships
- **Camila and Nicolas:** Their conflict is not personal at first, but each sees the other's priority as potentially dangerous. Completion optimization, merchant data requests, and consent language can turn them openly adversarial.
- **Matias and Nicolas:** Nicolas experiences technical caution as delay; Matias experiences commercial urgency as pressure to lie with nicer words.
- **Lucia and Rafaela:** Lucia needs a clean public story; Rafaela keeps surfacing messy garment truths that make the story harder to sell.
- **Lucia and Camila:** They both care about trust, but Lucia is more willing to manage ambiguity publicly. Camila may eventually read that as betrayal.

## Unspoken Structure
- Everyone knows Nicolas is making the company feel more real right now, but no one wants merchant access to become moral authority.
- Everyone knows Rafaela's manual work is holding the demo together more than the public story admits.
- Everyone knows Matias is right about many limits, but his correctness can become operationally unbearable.
- Everyone knows Lucia is carrying legitimacy none of them can replace, which makes it harder to challenge her when she reframes drift as strategy.
- Camila's concerns are often softened into "UX" or "trust language" because naming the deeper issue would force the company to admit how intimate the product really is.
- Avoidance happens through productivity: calls, pilot prep, interface tweaks, catalog cleanup, and demo polishing absorb arguments that should probably be explicit.

## Group-Level Asymmetries
- **Who wants the company most:** Lucia wants the company as a category-making project; Nicolas wants it as proof that speed and commercial realism can win.
- **Who needs it most:** Nicolas needs near-term commercial proof; Rafaela needs the company to recognize work that is usually invisible; Lucia needs the company to justify the scale of her ambition.
- **Who could survive leaving:** Matias could likely find technical work elsewhere; Camila could move into product, research, or trust roles; Nicolas could sell for another commerce company. Leaving would be survivable, but each would lose the specific leverage Tallea gives them.
- **Who has more symbolic power than operational power:** Lucia, especially when public attention rises. Her meaning-making can outrun what the team can execute.
- **Who has more operational power than symbolic power:** Rafaela, because the product depends on her catalog and garment judgment more than outsiders can see.
- **Who has situational power:** Camila gains power during privacy, consent, and user-drop-off crises; Nicolas gains power during runway or pilot pressure; Matias gains power when a technical failure becomes visible.

## Likely Early Fracture Points
- A merchant asks for a white-label pilot that would hide Tallea from users and give the merchant more control over fit-profile data.
- A demo or newsletter mention creates inbound attention that assumes broader automation than the team can support.
- A high-confidence recommendation fails on denim, trousers, or a fitted dress and becomes emotionally charged for a user.
- Nicolas pushes for a paid pilot before Matias and Rafaela believe the catalog can be ingested safely.
- Camila blocks an onboarding shortcut that would improve completion but weaken informed consent.
- Lucia reframes a narrow merchant opportunity as strategic infrastructure, and the team cannot tell whether she is seeing the future or making peace with drift.

## What Makes This Group Worth Following
- They are not fighting because they misunderstand the company; they are fighting because each understands a different real version of it.
- Their conflicts are operational and emotional at the same time: a button label, pilot scope, catalog field, or confidence threshold can carry ambition, trust, money, and pride.
- The group has enough affection and mutual dependence to keep working after bruising conversations, but not enough alignment to make those conversations disappear.
- Their youth matters: they are capable, but still earning authority while external people begin treating them as representatives of something bigger.
- The company can plausibly become several things, and each future empowers a different member of the group.

## Summary for System Use
At Day 0, Tallea's core group functions through uncomfortable interdependence. Lucia holds narrative legitimacy, Matias protects technical truth, Camila guards user trust, Nicolas brings merchant access, and Rafaela anchors garment-data reality. The group stays viable because no one is redundant, but every major external pressure shifts internal power: pilots empower Nicolas, public attention empowers Lucia, technical limits empower Matias, privacy discomfort empowers Camila, and catalog mess empowers Rafaela. Early cycles should turn product claims, merchant pilots, consent choices, catalog cleanup, and white-label interest into internal conflict without breaking the group completely.
