# Core Protagonist Requirements

## Purpose
This document defines the narrative requirements for the protagonist core in a serial startup world.

It does not define specific characters, names, biographies, or fixed roles.
It defines the minimum human architecture needed to support attachment, conflict, continuity, and change.

Use it as a base for future character design, relationships, and internal group dynamics.

---

## 1. The Core Must Depend on Itself in Uncomfortable Ways

**Rule**
- The group members must truly need each other to act, decide, survive, or preserve capability.
- This dependence should be functional, but not comfortable.

**Why it matters**
- It explains why the group stays together despite conflict.
- It prevents easy or trivial separation.

**Use**
- Distribute non-redundant abilities, access, and legitimacy.
- Force cooperation between people who would prefer more independence.
- Make internal rupture costly.
- Allow wear and friction without making separation the default outcome.

**Avoid**
- weak interdependence
- total and rigid dependence

---

## 2. The Core Must Be Unevenly Distributed

**Rule**
- Capacity, vulnerability, desire, priority, and risk tolerance should not be evenly shared.
- No one should do everything, want the same thing, or fail for the same reason.

**Why it matters**
- Unevenness creates negotiation, resentment, admiration, dependence, and conflict.
- Different crises change who becomes central and who becomes exposed.

**Use**
- Give each member localized strengths and persistent limits.
- Distribute different fragilities across the group.
- Assign different priorities and appetites for risk.
- Let the same crisis affect each member differently.

**Avoid**
- overbalanced teams
- extreme and frozen inequality

---

## 3. Internal Politics Must Stay Alive

**Rule**
- Authority, influence, and legitimacy inside the group should never be fully settled.
- More than one basis for leadership should remain valid.

**Possible bases**
- competence
- vision
- sacrifice
- access
- experience
- charisma
- strategic judgment
- external relationships

**Why it matters**
- It keeps power conflict available without changing the setting.
- Different crises can redistribute centrality inside the group.

**Use**
- Treat power and legitimacy as mobile variables.
- Let different contexts favor different kinds of authority.
- Keep tension between formal authority and real influence.
- Allow alliances and internal reordering.

**Avoid**
- random shifts in authority
- permanently settled hierarchy

---

## 4. The Core Must Embody World Tensions

**Rule**
- Internal differences inside the group should express broader structural tensions in the world.
- Conflict should not be only about personality.

**Examples**
- principle vs convenience
- vision vs adaptation
- loyalty vs survival
- quality vs speed
- control vs growth
- identity vs function

**Why it matters**
- It makes each argument practical and thematic at the same time.
- The group becomes the human surface where the world becomes visible.

**Use**
- Give each future member a dominant structural tension.
- Re-activate those tensions in recurring decisions.
- Let the same problem produce opposing interpretations.
- Allow gradual shifts over time.

**Avoid**
- reducing each member to a single idea

---

## 5. Relationships Must Accumulate More Than They Resolve

**Rule**
- Relationships should carry affection, debt, admiration, exhaustion, rivalry, gratitude, shame, and resentment.
- They should not reset easily.

**Why it matters**
- It creates serial depth, subtext, and emotional payoff.
- Future scenes carry memory.

**Use**
- Model relationships as multi-vector, not binary.
- Track favors, injuries, debts, and moments of recognition.
- Allow deterioration, partial repair, and ambivalent accumulation.
- Keep explicit emotional confession rare and meaningful.

**Avoid**
- fast full resolution
- static subtext that never moves

---

## 6. The Core Must Survive Conflict Without Full Reset or Total Collapse

**Rule**
- The group must be able to absorb intense friction, partial breakage, provisional repair, and continued operation.
- Conflict must have consequences, but not always destroy the group.

**Why it matters**
- This supports long serial storytelling.
- The group can survive its own collisions without becoming repetitive.

**Use**
- Anchor ties in need, debt, habit, recognition, and residual affection.
- Avoid full emotional resets.
- Let the group keep operating while carrying fractures.
- Allow some fractures to reconfigure the core without ending it.

**Avoid**
- consequence-free damage
- groups that break too easily

---

## 7. Each Part Must Be Quickly Recognizable, but the Whole Must Stay Surprising

**Rule**
- Each member should have recognizable patterns of reaction and function.
- The combinations between them should still produce novelty.

**Why it matters**
- This creates attachment and readability without making the group mechanical.
- The audience learns each part, but cannot fully predict the interaction.

**Use**
- Define stable reactions to pressure, humiliation, opportunity, and fear.
- Keep a stable base with contextual variation.
- Build scenes where known patterns collide in new situations.
- Create surprise through interaction, not arbitrary personality changes.

**Avoid**
- rigid pattern loops
- causeless personality changes

---

## 8. External Pressure Must Become Internal Conflict

**Rule**
- Forces from the world should reorder the group, not only threaten it from outside.

**Why it matters**
- It multiplies the dramatic value of each external event.
- The world and the core stop feeling like separate layers.

**Use**
- Translate outside shocks into disagreement, guilt, power shifts, forced alignment, or uneasy cooperation.
- Let the same pressure hit each member differently.
- Vary the absorption mode: fight, paralysis, sacrifice, retreat, solidarity, displacement.

**Avoid**
- every outside event causing the same internal fight

---

## 9. The Core Must Generate Scene Energy Even in Ordinary Moments

**Rule**
- The internal dynamic should stay interesting even without a major crisis.

**Why it matters**
- It sustains continuity between peaks.
- The system does not depend only on big events.

**Use**
- Design differences in style, ego, language, timing, and sensitivity.
- Let small tasks carry subtext, hierarchy, and need.
- Use ordinary moments to surface larger tensions.
- Preserve gradation; not everything should explode.

**Avoid**
- a group that only works under major crisis
- constant overactivation

---

## 10. The Core Must Hold Damage, Humor, and Meaning at Once

**Rule**
- Interactions should be useful, uncomfortable, revealing, painful, and sometimes funny at the same time.

**Why it matters**
- This creates tone, texture, and memorability.
- It avoids excessive solemnity without removing stakes.

**Use**
- Design clashes of style, ego, timing, language, and sensitivity.
- Let humor come from human truth, not external jokes.
- Let funny scenes leave emotional or strategic residue.
- Use ridicule to reveal vulnerability, hierarchy, or self-deception.

**Avoid**
- humor that cancels cost
- damage that removes all lightness

---

## General Design Standard

The protagonist core should not be designed as:

- a harmonious group
- a simple set of functional roles

It should operate as a structure of:

- mutual need
- internal inequality
- unstable power
- accumulated history
- absorbing conflict

It should sustain, at the same time:

- cooperation and resentment
- usefulness and friction
- readability and surprise
- continuity and transformation
- humor and damage
- intimacy and structural dispute

---

## What This Document Is For

Use this document to:

- create the concrete protagonist characters
- define initial relationships and active tensions
- build a group dynamic that can last over time
- translate world forces into human conflict
- evaluate whether the group has enough serial density

This document does not yet define:

- names
- biographies
- specific roles
- individual arcs
- final group composition
