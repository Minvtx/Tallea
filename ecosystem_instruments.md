# Ecosystem Instruments

## Purpose
This document lists the main narrative instrument families available in a serial startup world.

These are not mandatory elements or a fixed checklist.
They are external forces that create pressure, validation, exposure, competition, capability shifts, and board reconfiguration.

Use them as a toolbox for the orchestrator agent.

## Core Rule
Each family can:

- appear or disappear depending on the system phase
- change intensity over time
- combine with other families
- act as opportunity, threat, judgment, accelerator, or distortion

---

## 1. Capital and Governance

**Includes**
- investors and capital providers
- runway, cash, liquidity
- board, oversight, control bodies

**Creates pressure**
- financial dependence
- reduced autonomy
- accountability
- growth pressure
- time pressure
- power shifts

**Enables conflict**
- control vs survival
- vision vs demanded results
- autonomy vs access
- operators vs supervisors
- disputes over terms, metrics, timing, and priorities

**How to use**
- Treat capital as conditional, never neutral.
- Let money solve one problem while creating another.
- Use cash as the system clock.
- Treat governance as political, not only administrative.
- Give financial actors distinct motives and styles.

**Avoid**
- capital as a clean solution
- flat villain investors

---

## 2. Market, Demand, and Validation

**Includes**
- users
- customers
- contracts
- adoption and churn
- metrics
- hype and virality

**Creates pressure**
- need for external validation
- unstable adoption
- delivery obligations
- gap between promise and real use
- constant measurement
- misreading signals

**Enables conflict**
- vision vs demand
- selling vs delivering
- growth vs stability
- intuition vs data
- hype vs readiness
- strategic customer vs product focus

**How to use**
- Treat the market as a semi-autonomous external reality.
- Separate attention, adoption, retention, revenue, and real usage.
- Use metrics as signals, not absolute truth.
- Let commercial wins create new burdens.
- Keep room for ambiguous interpretation.

**Avoid**
- a market that always confirms expectations
- pure chaos with no logic

---

## 3. Perception, Reputation, and Amplification

**Includes**
- media and press
- peer community
- sector scene
- backlash and scandal
- expert validation
- informal prestige

**Creates pressure**
- public exposure
- mismatch between reality and narrative
- reputation acceleration
- status anxiety
- visible judgment
- pressure to perform identity

**Enables conflict**
- internal truth vs external image
- exploit hype vs correct it
- confidentiality vs visibility
- belong vs stand apart
- unfair or exaggerated coverage
- conflict over public representation

**How to use**
- Separate formal reputation from informal reputation.
- Coverage does not need to match real importance.
- Use rumors, reviews, scandals, and endorsements as amplifiers.
- Connect perception to talent, capital, customers, and internal power.
- Let reputation change real options.

**Avoid**
- reputation that only punishes
- decorative public perception with no material effect

---

## 4. Competition and External Mirrors

**Includes**
- direct competitors
- narrative rivals
- alternative actors in the same space

**Creates pressure**
- constant comparison
- compressed timelines
- replacement risk
- identity pressure
- forced reactions

**Enables conflict**
- copy vs differentiate
- paranoia vs strategy
- speed vs integrity
- uncomfortable admiration for a rival
- questions about what makes the system distinct

**How to use**
- Design rivals as plausible alternative paths.
- Give them real strengths and internal logic.
- Let them affect timing, morale, and public perception.
- Use them as identity mirrors, not only threats.
- Allow rivalry, imitation, tactical alliance, or mutual contamination.

**Avoid**
- weak rivals
- omnipotent rivals
- purely evil rivals

---

## 5. Material Capacity and Technical Dependence

**Includes**
- product and technology
- technical debt
- infrastructure
- external platforms
- talent and retention

**Creates pressure**
- friction between ambition and reality
- technical or operational limits
- dependence on external systems
- scaling cost
- execution fragility
- loss or gain of real capability

**Enables conflict**
- build well vs build fast
- independence vs leverage
- hiring for need vs culture
- patch vs rewrite
- promise vs delivery
- current dependence vs future risk

**How to use**
- Treat technology as materialized history, not neutral tooling.
- Let past decisions constrain future options.
- Treat talent as actors with agency and cultural effects.
- Let capability gains introduce debt or disruption.
- Combine this family with market, cash, or reputation for cascades.

**Avoid**
- convenient failures with no prior logic
- unlimited capacity

---

## 6. Institutional Frameworks and Enabling Structures

**Includes**
- regulation
- legal system
- compliance
- accelerators and incubators
- support programs
- partners
- strategic allies
- anchor clients

**Creates pressure**
- formalization
- adaptation to external rules
- opportunities with costs
- institutional dependence
- premature scaling pressure
- contractual or regulatory asymmetry

**Enables conflict**
- speed vs prudence
- autonomy vs access
- identity vs adaptation
- large opportunity vs external capture
- improvisation vs compliance
- real help vs performative progress

**How to use**
- Treat institutions as ambiguous enablers.
- Use them to introduce deadlines, obligations, filters, and mediation.
- Let institutional access affect reputation and operations.
- Let positive frameworks still change the system rhythm.
- Keep institutional incentives only partially aligned with the startup.

**Avoid**
- institutions as pure obstacles
- institutions as magic validation doors

---

## 7. Sensitive Information, Secrecy, and Leaks

**Includes**
- intellectual property
- leaks
- confidential information
- public promises that are hard to retract
- strategic knowledge flows

**Creates pressure**
- need to hide and reveal at the same time
- fear of copying or exposure
- legal and reputational vulnerability
- visibility vs protection
- unequal power through access to information

**Enables conflict**
- how much to reveal
- internal or external suspicion
- leak-driven crises
- trust vs control
- secrecy as strategy

**How to use**
- Treat sensitive information as a resource.
- Let sharing or withholding it create both cost and benefit.
- Use leaks to trigger media attention, rivalry, legal action, or internal conflict.
- Allow over-secrecy to cause damage too.
- Connect secrecy to trust, timing, and reputation.

**Avoid**
- constant leaks
- consequence-free disclosure

---

## 8. Public Events as Ecosystem Condensers

**Includes**
- demos
- launches
- conferences
- pitch days
- public presentations
- ritualized exposure moments

**Creates pressure**
- visible deadlines
- performance under judgment
- public comparison
- concentrated attention
- fast reconfiguration of the field

**Enables conflict**
- preparation under pressure
- disputes over what to show or promise
- human or technical failure at the worst time
- sudden reputation change
- simultaneous activation of multiple instruments

**How to use**
- Treat events as dense nodes where several forces converge.
- Show preparation and long consequences.
- Allow good, bad, or ambiguous outcomes.
- Use events to connect market, media, investors, rivalry, reputation, and capability.
- Reserve them for high-density moments.

**Avoid**
- all events ending the same way
- empty routine events with no cost

---

## Practical Use for Agents

When generating startup-world events:

- pick only the instrument families that matter now
- vary intensity instead of activating everything at once
- make gains create new constraints
- make pressure concrete and material
- keep external forces legible but not fully predictable
